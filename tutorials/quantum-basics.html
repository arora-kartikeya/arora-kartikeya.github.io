Introduction

Quantum computing represents a paradigm shift in how we process information. Unlike classical computers that use bits as the smallest unit of information, quantum computers use quantum bits or qubits. This tutorial introduces the fundamental concepts of quantum computing, focusing on the properties that make quantum systems unique and powerful.

Classical vs. Quantum Bits

A classical bit can exist in one of two states: 0 or 1. These states form the foundation of all classical computing, from simple calculations to complex algorithms.

In contrast, a qubit can exist in a state that is a combination of both 0 and 1 simultaneously. This is mathematically represented as:

|\psi⟩ = \alpha|0⟩ + \beta|1⟩

Where:

  • |\psi⟩ represents the quantum state
  • |0⟩ and |1⟩ are the basis states (analogous to classical 0 and 1)
  • \alpha and \beta are complex numbers called amplitudes
  • |\alpha|² + |\beta|² = 1 (probability normalization)

Note: The notation |0⟩ and |1⟩ is called Dirac or bra-ket notation and is standard in quantum mechanics.

Superposition

Superposition is a fundamental quantum property that allows qubits to exist in multiple states simultaneously. This is not just a theoretical construct but a physical reality at the quantum level.

For example, a qubit in an equal superposition state is represented as:

|\psi⟩ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|0⟩ + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|1⟩

This means the qubit has a 50% probability of being measured as |0⟩ and a 50% probability of being measured as |1⟩.

Example: Hadamard Gate

The Hadamard gate (H) is a quantum gate that creates superposition. When applied to |0⟩, it creates an equal superposition:

H|0⟩ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|0⟩ + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|1⟩

Entanglement

Quantum entanglement is a phenomenon where two or more qubits become correlated in such a way that the quantum state of each qubit cannot be described independently of the others, regardless of the distance separating them.

For example, a two-qubit entangled state called a Bell state is represented as:

|\Phi^+⟩ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|00⟩ + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|11⟩

In this state, if we measure the first qubit and find it in state |0⟩, we know with certainty that the second qubit is also in state |0⟩. Similarly, if the first qubit is measured as |1⟩, the second qubit must be in state |1⟩.

Important: Einstein referred to entanglement as "spooky action at a distance" because the correlation between entangled qubits appears to violate the principle that information cannot travel faster than light.

Quantum Measurement

When we measure a qubit, we force it to assume one of its basis states (|0⟩ or |1⟩). This process is probabilistic and causes the collapse of the superposition.

For a qubit in state |\psi⟩ = \alpha|0⟩ + \beta|1⟩:

  • The probability of measuring |0⟩ is |\alpha|²
  • The probability of measuring |1⟩ is |\beta|²

After measurement, the qubit's state collapses to the measured basis state, losing all information about its previous superposition.

Conclusion

Understanding qubits, superposition, entanglement, and measurement provides the foundation for quantum computing. These principles enable quantum algorithms to solve certain problems exponentially faster than classical algorithms.
